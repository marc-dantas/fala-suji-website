Fala Suji - Documentation

How Fala Suji works?

Fala Suji works in a very similar way to Toki Pona. The main thing that makes it possible to express things in Fala Suji is the grammatical class of modifiers, which work like adjectives/adverbs for just about any other word in Fala Suji. The modifier is the word that makes it possible to express any idea. This has already been explored by Sonja Lang in her linguistic work. Translating from a common language to Fala Suji (or Toki Pona) works by “taking apart” words to a more abstract, less specific meaning that can be interpreted in a variety of ways. Thus, only the context specifies its meaning. For example, if you want to translate the word “assassin” to Toki Pona or Fala Suji, you need to “disassemble” the word to its basic meaning. And what would be? If you thought of "person who kills someone" or just "person who kills", you are correct. Thus it is possible to translate the word “assassin” to Fala Suji or Toki Pona.

Beginning

Like Toki Pona, this language has a very simple phonetics and phonology similar to Portuguese. The consonants and vowels (as in Sonja Lang's work) are practically identical to Portuguese. With the exception of the letter J, which sounds similar to the pronunciation of the letter Y (epsilon) in English or /j/ in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA).

Grammar

Fala Suji's grammar is relatively simple, has few rules and works simply. But first, let's contextualize all the grammatical classes that Fala Suji has.

  • Starting with
    Noun
    : Nouns are simply names, words that represent a name for something, such as a flower, sunflower, car, person, etc. Names do not have inner classes.
  • Pronoun
    : Pronouns are words that represents a reference to something, in this case, only the equivalents of personal and possessive pronouns, being all of them: ma (you), me (he/she/it) and mi (I).
  • Conjunction
    : Conjunctions are words that compare one or more things, namely the words "e" (and), "o" (or) and "soma" (is equal to).
  • Verb
    : It is a word that indicates action. It can be in the present, past or future. There are two types of verb forms:
    Transitive verb
    forms, are those that need a complement for the action to be coherent and the
    Intransitive verb
    , that do not need a complement.
  • Conjunction
    : Conjunctions are words that compare one or more things, namely the words e (and), o (or) and soma (is equal to).
  • Separator
    : Word that separates the parts of the sentence. There are two types of separators, those between subject and verb (
    Subject-verb separator
    ), those that separate the subject and the main verb form and those between the verb and the object (
    Verb-object separator
    ), those that delimit where the verbal part of the sentence ends and the object begins.
  • Modifier
    : Modifiers are crucial words for such a small language to work. Basically, modifiers are adjectives or adverbs that change the meaning of a name or other words in general.
  • Numeral
    : Numeral is the word that counts things.
  • Object
    : Object is simply anything that represent a subject or things in general.
  • Particle
    : This word serves to delimit parts of the sentence, remember that it is not the same thing as a separator, it works in a more “unary” way, it only delimits parts of the sentence. There are four types of particles: the
    Interrogative particle
    , the one that initiates a question or is used as "why". It is the word iso. The
    Reason particle
    is the one that gives reason to a previous statement, being the word pose. The
    Option particle
    has the same function as “which” or “what” in English, being the word kajo. There is also the
    Equivalence particle
    , which is the one that indicates the complete or partial equivalence of something or someone, being the word soma.

Now that we have defined the affirmations and the questions. Now I'm going to show you some example sentences in Fala Suji and I want you to look at them and see how simple they are.

  • Iso mi muto ike tado a dijo muto? (Are we so different? / Why are we so different?)
  • Ma muto ike tado a dijo. (You are different)
  • Mi e ma ike tado a me muto. (I and you are them)

Note that these sentences are easily understandable (if you don't understand them, try to consult the vocabulary chapter and the definitions of the grammatical classes).
