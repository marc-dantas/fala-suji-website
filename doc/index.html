Fala Suji - Documentation

How Fala Suji works?

Fala Suji works in a very similar way to Toki Pona. Like Toki Pona, the sentences are not capitalized, unless it starts with a name. The main thing that makes it possible to express things in Fala Suji is the grammatical class of modifiers, which work like adjectives/adverbs for just about any other word in Fala Suji. The modifier is the word that makes it possible to express any idea. This has already been explored by Sonja Lang in her linguistic work. Translating from a common language to Fala Suji (or Toki Pona) works by “taking apart” words to a more abstract, less specific meaning that can be interpreted in a variety of ways. Thus, only the context specifies its meaning. For example, if you want to translate the word “assassin” to Toki Pona or Fala Suji, you need to “disassemble” the word to its basic meaning. And what would be? If you thought of "person who kills someone" or just "person who kills", you are correct. Thus it is possible to translate the word “assassin” to Fala Suji or Toki Pona.

Beginning

Like Toki Pona, this language has a very simple phonetics and phonology similar to Portuguese. The consonants and vowels (as in Sonja Lang's work) are practically identical to Portuguese. With the exception of the letter J, which sounds similar to the pronunciation of the letter Y (epsilon) in English or /j/ in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA).

Grammar

Fala Suji's grammar is relatively simple, has few rules and works simply. But first, let's contextualize all the grammatical classes that Fala Suji has.

  • Starting with
    Noun
    : Nouns are simply names, words that represent a name for something, such as a flower, sunflower, car, person, etc. Names do not have inner classes.
  • Pronoun
    : Pronouns are words that represents a reference to something, in this case, only the equivalents of personal and possessive pronouns, being all of them: ma (you), me (he/she/it) and mi (I).
  • Verb
    : It is a word that indicates action. It can be in the present, past or future. There are two types of verb forms: Transitive verb forms, are those that need a complement for the action to be coherent and the Intransitive verb, that do not need a complement.
  • Conjunction
    : Conjunctions are words that compare one or more things, namely the words e (and), o (or) and soma (is equal to).
  • Separator
    : Word that separates the parts of the sentence. There are two types of separators, those between subject and verb (Subject-verb separator), those that separate the subject and the main verb form and those between the verb and the object (Verb-object separator), those that delimit where the verbal part of the sentence ends and the object begins.
  • Modifier
    : Modifiers are crucial words for such a small language to work. Basically, modifiers are adjectives or adverbs that change the meaning of a name or other words in general.
  • Numeral
    : Numeral is the word that counts things.
  • Object
    : Object is simply anything that represent a subject or things in general.
  • Particle
    : This word serves to delimit parts of the sentence, remember that it is not the same thing as a separator, it works in a more “unary” way, it only delimits parts of the sentence. There are five types of particles: the Interrogative particle, the one that initiates a question or is used as "why". It is the word iso. The Reason particle is the one that gives reason to a previous statement, being the word pose. The Option particle has the same function as “which” or “what” in English, being the word kajo. There is also the Equivalence particle, which is the one that indicates the complete or partial equivalence of something or someone, being the word soma and there's the Destination particle, the one that represents a destination (real or not) to a previous statement, being represented by the word ajo.
  • Phrase
    : Any sentence in Fala Suji.

Forming the plural and... What about gender?

The plural in Fala Suji is formed from the modifier muto (very, much, many etc.). It can be used in nouns and pronouns to imply that there is more than one of something. See the following examples and note the relationship between the word muto and the plural words in English.

  • mi muto ike tado a penoja muto bono
    • (Correct) We are good people
    • (Wrong) "Many of me" are good "many person"
  • me muto ike ojo a muno
    • (Correct) They look (are looking) at the moon
    • (Wrong) "Many of he/she/it" look (are looking) at the moon

The plural of Fala Suji is very abstract. But with enough interpretation, it is possible to understand what the speaker is trying to say.

In this language, there's not any type of gender. All words has a undefined gender, since this language doesn't have articles.

Verbal forms

In the previous chapter you saw the syntax of questions and statements and had a brief introduction to verbs. In this chapter you will explore verbal concepts in more depth.

Every verb in Fala Suji can only be a verb if and only if there is a separator between the subject and the verb: ike, ika, iko. Which conjugate the verb in a given tense: present, past and future, respectively, and (in the case of transitive verbs) the separator a that we will delve into in the next content.

This explanation was not for nothing, as we are going to get into the classification of indirect verbs.

Indirect verbs are transitive verbs that share the meaning with the separator a and make it a specific preposition, for example the transitive verbal form ike luda (to play in the present tense) needs a preposition that indicates what or who to play with: Who plays, plays with something (preposition with). This preposition “role" is passed to the a separator in this type of verb.

Look at some examples with this and other indirect verbs and see their respective translation.

  • Verb iko nena ("Reach the top" in the future tense)
    • mi iko nena a nena (I will reach the top of the mountain)
  • Verb ike luda (Play in the future tense)
    • mi ike luda a luda mi (I play with my toy)

You may have already noticed the connection of the separator a with the prepositions of the English language. Now let's get into one more subject in verbs: verb modification. Verbal modification is the use of modifiers on verbs and changing their meaning, the most common is using the word nene to negate a verb.

Let's see some examples with this word modifying the verb form.

  • Verb ike tado (Be in the present tense)
    • mi ike tado nene a dita! (I am not a fool!)
    • me muto ike tado nene a nima muto! (They are not animals)

The use of nene is a modification, as mentioned in the previous chapter. Now we will see the use of other modifiers and create other verbs using the modifiers.

  • Verb ike ojo + Modifier noto = "Look from far"
    • mi ike ojo noto a ma (I watch you)
  • Verb ike fala + Modifier deto = "Speak inside"
    • mi ike fala deto a ma (I think about you.)

Now you can see how interesting things get in this kind of language with few words.

It is possible to express practically anything just by combining words. This is only possible thanks to modifiers.

Let's explore the concept of verbal expressions. The verb separator i is used to introduce new verbs and thus compose a verbal expression. See the examples where this separator is used.

  • mi ike kere i do a sijo (I want to do this/that)

Like the word "to" in English, the i separator works by making the verb infinitive tense.

As in English, not all verbs work as introducers of phrases, they work as linking verbs, but this classification does not exist in Fala Suji.

Now we will get into the verb endings. They serve to indicate which is the subject in ellipsis in the sentence: mi, me, ma, mi muto, me muto and ma muto. Use the apostrophe (') and then the following suffixes:

  • mi (I): 'i
  • me (He/She/It): 'e
  • ma (You): 'a
  • mi muto (We): 'io
  • me muto (They): 'eo
  • ma muto (You): 'ao

These suffixes are put after the verb itself. If the last vowel of the verb is the same as the vowel of the ending, it is not necessary to pronounce the last vowel of the verb to avoid pronunciation problems. Attention: The use of this feature is not mandatory. This is a more informal feature. Let's look at some examples and see how it is applied.

  • ike tado'i nene a dita (I am not a fool)
  • ike tado'e a nima (He/She/It is a/the animal)

Particles

Particles are words that function as prepositions at specific places in sentences. This chapter will explain how the five particles of Fala Suji work.

Let's start with the simplest, the interrogative particle. As the name implies, it is used to ask a question to someone, identifying it by the word iso.

Look at some examples below.

  • iso ma ike tado a penoja bono? (Are you a good person?)
  • iso me muto ike more i luda? (Do they like to play?)
  • iso Barasili ike tado a teja bono? (Is Brazil a good country?)

The iso particle is fixed, that is, it can only be located in a specific place in a sentence. Being graphically represented by:

iso ____________

Now let's take a look at the reason particle. It serves to give a reason to the previous sentence, like the word because in English. It is represented by the word pose.

See the examples.

  • Barasili ike tado a teja bono pose mi ike more i kasa a Barasili (Brazil is a good country because I like to live in Brazil)
  • ma ike kasa a kasa mi pose ma ike jo nene a kasa. (You live in my house because you don't have one.)

pose's also a fixed particle. Being represented by:

____________ pose ____________

pose ____________

Fala Suji has the option particle. It works like "which" or "what" in English. Look at the examples.

  • iso kajo* nomo ike tado a ma? (What name is yours? / What's your name?)
  • iso ma ike joja a kajo* teja? (You are from what/which place/country?)

* This particle can not be followed by verbs or pronouns, only names.

kajo isn't considered a fixed particle. But can be represented by:

____________ kajo [__________]

kajo [__________] ____________

The equivalence particle has the same function of the word "as" or "like" in English. It's represented by the word soma.

Look at the examples.

  • mi ike tado a ojo bono soma ma (I'm beautiful like you)
  • Barasili ike tado a teja bono soma Esapana! (Brazil is a good country like Spain!)

This particle is fixed. Representation:

__________ soma [__________]

Let's finish with the destination particle. It is like "to", "for", "in", "on", or "at" in English. See the examples to understand better this particle.

  • mi ike jiro a kasa mi ajo ike ojo a FWC (I'm going to my house to watch the FWC.)
  • mi ike tado a penoja bono pose mi ike kere i jo a vere ajo vida. (I'm a good person because I want to win everything in life.

ajo's a fixed particle:

____________ ajo ____________

Numerals

Numerals are used to count the quantity of something. They come before the object to be numbered and can be used as a “modifier” for some words. Take a look at the example below.

  • to penoja ike tado a mi e ma (The duo is me and you / The duo is mine and yours)

Ordinal numbers in Fala Suji are made by using the word numo after a numeral. Look at the example.

  • to numo gupu ike tado a gupu gano
    • (Wrong translation) Two groups are big groups
    • (Correct translation) The second group is a big group

Now we will cover how to speak numbers greater than nine (9). To say, for example, ten (10) you have to use the teno modifier to add a zero to the number, so ten in Fala Suji is no teno. There is also the mino modifier to add three zeros to the number. This is applicable for all other numbers:

  • no teno: Ten (1 + 0 = 10)
  • to teno teno: Two hundred (2 + 00 = 200)
  • no teno e te: Thirteen (1 + 0 and 3 = 13)
  • no mino e no: A thousand and one (1 + 000 and 1 = 1001)

Informal language

Informal language is present in any language, and it is strongly present in Fala Suji. It has the main function of decreasing the size of sentences, which can become very large due to chaining words using modifiers.

The main thing that is removed in the informal sentences in Fala Suji is the verb to be (tado). See some examples to understand it better.

  • mi ike (tado) a penoja bono! (I'm a good person!)
  • ma ike (tado) nene a dita! (You're not a fool!)

Now let's talk about incomplete action claims. Which simply serves to replace the word “yes” and use as an answer to a question. See a simple made-up dialogue between two people and see the elements that are missing.

  • iso ma ike a Johono? (Are you John?)
  • mi ike (I am [yes])
  • iso ike joja'a a kajo teja? (Where are you from?)
  • mi ike joja a Barasili (I'm from Brazil)
  • iso Barasili ike a teja bono? (Is Brazil a good country?)
  • Barasili ike! (Brazil is!)

Note that the removal of the verb to be is constant, it is quite informal, but it is very common. It is also valid to use the endings in the words ike, ika and iko. For example: ike'i nene a dita! (I'm not a fool!)

Porting names to Fala Suji (the falasujization)

Most phonemes in English names are allowed in Fala Suji (see dictionary in vocabulary chapter). Basically, only consonant clusters and alone vowels are not allowed. Which can be removed by adding the closest vowel to the consonants and adding the letter H to let the vowel with a consonant. Look at the examples.

  • Michael: Micahahele
  • Mark: Maraka
  • Aaron: Aharono
  • James: Jamese
  • Clara: Calara
  • Sophia: Sopahiha
  • Leonardo: Lehonarado
  • Frederick: Feredericiki
  • Oswald: Osowalada
  • Yiara*: Ihara

* In this case, there is a phoneme that does not exist in Fala Suji. Thus, it becomes the most similar phoneme.

In the case of country or state names. It is necessary to use the name written in the native language of the land or the english name and translate it to Fala Suji. Removing accents (if any) and adapting phonemes.

These names work as modifiers for nouns. Thus, to represent the name of countries, the noun teja is used. For names of people who are from a certain nation, the noun penoja is used. For languages, fala is used.

  • Spain / España: Sapahini / Esapana
  • Brazil / Brasil: Barazili / Barasili
  • Portugal / Portugal: Porotugala
  • Greece / Ελλάδα [Elláda]: Gerehece / Elelada
  • France / France: Faranace
  • Russia / Россия [Rossiya]: Rususiha / Rososiya
  • Japan / 日本 [Nihon]: Japana / Nihono
  • Egypt / مِصر [Miṣr]: Egeyepete / Misiri
  • ...

Take a look at some examples using these adapted names.

  • mi ike tado a penoja Esapana (I'm spanish)
  • ike fala'io a fala Enegelanada e fala suji (We speak English and Fala Suji)
  • ma ike tado a penoja Rososiya (You are Russian)

Common expressions and interjections

This chapter will deal with expressions to say, for example, "hi", "bye", "thank you", "no problem", etc. Interjections, which serve to demonstrate an expression of emotion, will also be explored.

Let's start with basic expressions and the translations

  • Hi / Hello: ike fala
  • Bye / Goodbye: ike jiro'i
  • Please: mi ike kere
  • Thanks / Thank you: ike more'i
  • No problem / You're welcome (answer to "thank you"): ike jo nene a kene
  • Welcome: ike joja a bono
